First, the honest truth

Before we build a single trading rule, you deserve the uncomfortable data. Day trading is not a coin flip with even odds. The large studies that actually tracked real traders over years tell a remarkably consistent story: most people lose money, and the few winners are rare enough that you should assume, by default, that you are not one of them.

  • Barber, Lee, Liu & Odean studied every day trader in Taiwan from 1992 to 2006. More than 8 in 10 (over 80%) lost money net of fees, and only about 1% were reliably profitable after costs.
  • Chague, De-Losso & Giovannetti followed roughly 20,000 new Brazilian day traders. Among those who persisted more than 300 days, 97% lost money and only 1.1% earned more than the Brazilian minimum wage. Crucially, they found no evidence that traders learned and improved over time.
  • Barber & Odean found that the most active individual investors underperform the market by roughly 10 percentage points per year after costs.

Why the average trader must lose

This is not bad luck; it is arithmetic. For every share you buy, someone sells; for every winner, there is a loser. Before costs, trading is roughly zero-sum across all participants. But every trade leaks money to fees, the bid-ask spread (the gap between the buying and selling price), and taxes. That turns the game negative-sum. If the average outcome before costs is zero, then after the "house edge" of costs the average trader's expected return is negative:

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{net return}] = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\text{gross return}]}_{\approx\,0\text{ on average}} - \underbrace{\text{costs}}_{>\,0} \;<\; 0$$

Here \(\mathbb{E}[\cdot]\) is the expected value from intro stats: the long-run average over many trades. A small, persistent minority do show genuine skill that overcomes this edge, but they are the exception, not the rule.

Key idea

The success stories you hear are a survivorship-biased sample. We only see the winners who kept playing; the 80-97% who quit after losing are invisible. Selecting on "people who got rich trading" is like measuring only the lottery winners and concluding lottery tickets are a great investment.

Reality check

The realistic expected outcome of active day trading is to lose money. This project's goal is not to get rich. It is to understand the statistics, to learn safely on paper money (no real dollars at risk), and to respect how steep the odds genuinely are.

Moving-Average Crossover: Riding the Trend

The simplest trend-following idea is this: instead of reacting to every jittery price tick, first smooth the price, then bet that whatever direction it is sliding in will continue. A moving average is exactly that smoother — it is the running mean of recent closing prices, and like any sample mean, it averages out the noise so the signal underneath becomes visible.

Two ways to average

The Simple Moving Average over a window of \(N\) days weights the last \(N\) closing prices equally:

$$\mathrm{SMA}_t^{(N)} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=0}^{N-1} P_{t-i}$$

Here \(P_t\) is the closing price on day \(t\), and \(N\) is the window length (how many days we average). This is just the sample mean from intro stats, slid forward one day at a time.

The Exponential Moving Average instead gives more weight to recent prices, with a smoothing factor \(\alpha = \tfrac{2}{N+1}\):

$$\mathrm{EMA}_t = \alpha P_t + (1-\alpha)\,\mathrm{EMA}_{t-1}$$

Unrolling this recursion, today's price gets weight \(\alpha\), yesterday's \(\alpha(1-\alpha)\), the day before \(\alpha(1-\alpha)^2\), and so on — the weights decay exponentially into the past. Older prices never fully vanish; they just fade. A larger \(N\) means a smaller \(\alpha\), so the EMA reacts more slowly.

The crossover signal

Run a short average (window \(N_s\), e.g. 50 days) and a long one (window \(N_l\), e.g. 200 days), with \(N_s < N_l\), and watch their gap:

$$D_t = \mathrm{MA}^{(N_s)}_t - \mathrm{MA}^{(N_l)}_t$$

The short average tracks recent momentum; the long one tracks the established baseline. When the gap flips sign, the trend has turned. A golden cross (bullish — go long) is \(D_{t-1} \le 0 < D_t\); a death cross (bearish — exit or short) is \(D_{t-1} \ge 0 > D_t\).

Key idea

A moving average smooths noise to reveal the underlying trend; the crossover of a fast and a slow average detects the moment that trend changes direction.

Watch out

Averages lag by construction — they describe the past. Signals therefore arrive after a move has begun, and in flat, sideways markets the two averages tangle around each other, firing rapid buy/sell whipsaws that bleed money to fees and bad fills.

Mean Reversion with Bollinger Bands and the z-score

Some prices wander away from their recent average and then drift back, like a stretched rubber band snapping toward its resting length. A mean-reversion strategy bets on exactly that snap-back: when a price looks unusually high, sell (short); when it looks unusually low, buy (long); and close the position once the price returns to "normal." The whole strategy rests on one statistic you already know from intro stats: the z-score.

The recent average and its spread

We don't compare today's price to all history; we use a rolling window of the last \(N\) prices (default \(N=20\) trading days). Let \(P_t\) be the price at time \(t\). The rolling mean (the "middle band") is just the average of those recent prices:

$$\mu_t = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=0}^{N-1} P_{t-i}$$

The rolling standard deviation measures how spread out those recent prices were around their average. We use the population convention (dividing by \(N\), not \(N-1\)):

$$\sigma_t = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=0}^{N-1}(P_{t-i}-\mu_t)^2}$$

Here \(\mu_t\) is the rolling mean, and each \((P_{t-i}-\mu_t)^2\) is one squared deviation from that mean — the same recipe for standard deviation you computed by hand in statistics class, just over a sliding 20-day window.

Bollinger Bands are z-score lines in disguise

Bollinger Bands draw a channel a fixed number of standard deviations \(k\) (default \(k=2\)) above and below the mean:

\(\text{Upper}=\mu_t+k\sigma_t\), \(\quad\text{Lower}=\mu_t-k\sigma_t\).

The z-score tells us how many standard deviations the current price sits from the recent average:

$$z_t = \frac{P_t - \mu_t}{\sigma_t}$$

This is identical to the z-score from intro stats: subtract the mean, divide by the standard deviation. Notice the bands are nothing more than z-score contours — price touching the upper band means exactly \(z_t = +k\), and touching the lower band means \(z_t = -k\). The bands and the z-score are two views of the same number.

Key idea

The z-score \(z_t\) answers "how unusual is today's price, in standard-deviation units, relative to the last \(N\) days?" Mean reversion bets that extreme z-scores shrink back toward 0.

The trading rules

  • Enter LONG (buy) when \(z_t \le -z_{\text{entry}}\): the price is unusually cheap.
  • Enter SHORT (sell) when \(z_t \ge +z_{\text{entry}}\): the price is unusually expensive.
  • EXIT when price reverts to the mean (\(z_t \to 0\)): the rubber band has snapped back, so we take the profit.
Watch out

This entire strategy assumes the price series actually mean-reverts. In a strong trend, that assumption breaks: "cheap" keeps getting cheaper and "expensive" keeps getting more expensive. A falling stock can sit at \(z_t = -2\) for weeks while you keep buying into the decline. Mean reversion quietly bleeds money in trending markets — it only works when prices genuinely oscillate around a stable level.

The Relative Strength Index (RSI)

RSI is a momentum oscillator: a single number between 0 and 100 that asks "of all the price movement over the recent past, what fraction of it was upward?" If a stock has been rising relentlessly, RSI sits near 100; if it has been falling, near 0; if gains and losses roughly balance, near 50. It is the bot's gauge of whether a name has been pushed too far, too fast.

We start from the daily change \(\Delta_t = P_t - P_{t-1}\), where \(P_t\) is the closing price on day \(t\). We split each change into an up move and a down move, keeping losses as positive numbers:

$$U_t = \max(\Delta_t, 0), \qquad D_t = \max(-\Delta_t, 0).$$

On any given day exactly one of these is nonzero. We then smooth each series with Wilder's average over a window of \(N=14\) days. This is a running weighted average where the old average is carried forward and the newest value nudges it:

$$\overline{U}_t = \frac{(N-1)\,\overline{U}_{t-1} + U_t}{N}, \qquad \overline{D}_t = \frac{(N-1)\,\overline{D}_{t-1} + D_t}{N}.$$

Finally we form the relative strength \(\mathrm{RS}_t\), the ratio of average gain to average loss, and squash it into the 0–100 range:

$$\mathrm{RS}_t = \frac{\overline{U}_t}{\overline{D}_t}, \qquad \mathrm{RSI}_t = 100 - \frac{100}{1 + \mathrm{RS}_t}.$$

The transform is just rescaling: when average gains and losses are equal, \(\mathrm{RS}=1\) and \(\mathrm{RSI}=50\). At the edges, if there were no losses (\(\overline{D}_t = 0\)) RSI is defined as 100; if there were no gains (\(\overline{U}_t = 0\)) it is 0. So RSI is genuinely the share of recent movement that pointed up.

The bot reads it as a mean-reversion signal: RSI < 30 flags an oversold name (a candidate to buy), RSI > 70 flags an overbought one (sell or short), and a cross back through the 50 midline is a common exit.

Key idea

Wilder smoothing is an exponential moving average with weight \(\alpha = 1/N\) on the newest point. Do not confuse this with the ordinary EMA factor \(2/(N+1)\). For \(N=14\) that is \(\alpha = 1/14 \approx 0.071\) versus \(2/15 \approx 0.133\) — nearly double. Plugging the wrong \(\alpha\) into your code gives a faster, twitchier RSI that silently disagrees with every charting platform.

Watch out

The 30/70 thresholds are conventions, not laws. In a strong uptrend RSI can stay above 70 for weeks while the price keeps climbing — "overbought" is not a guarantee of reversal. Treat RSI as one piece of evidence, never a standalone trigger.

Pairs Trading: Betting on a Relationship, Not a Direction

The strategies so far bet on where a single stock is going. Pairs trading does something subtler: it finds two stocks that tend to move together and bets that any temporary gap between them will close. You stay roughly market-neutral (long one, short the other), so a broad market crash hurts both legs and largely cancels out. This is the realm of statistical arbitrage, and it leans hard on regression and a special hypothesis test.

Step 1: Find the hedge ratio with regression

Take two related stocks, A and B (say, two pipeline companies). Run an ordinary least-squares (OLS) regression of A's price on B's price:

$$A_t = \alpha + \beta B_t + \epsilon_t$$

Here \(A_t\) and \(B_t\) are the prices at time \(t\), \(\alpha\) is the intercept, \(\epsilon_t\) is the residual (the leftover wiggle), and \(\beta\) is the slope. This is exactly the line-fitting you saw in intro stats. The slope estimate is

$$\hat{\beta} = \frac{\sum_t (A_t-\bar A)(B_t-\bar B)}{\sum_t (B_t-\bar B)^2}$$

where \(\bar A,\bar B\) are the sample means. The \(\hat{\beta}\) tells you how many shares of B to trade against one share of A.

Step 2: Build the spread

The spread is just the regression residual — the part of A not explained by B:

$$s_t = A_t - \hat{\beta} B_t$$
Key idea

Each stock may wander aimlessly on its own, yet a well-chosen combination \(A - \hat{\beta}B\) can be stable, hovering around a fixed mean. When that holds, the two are called cointegrated. You're not predicting prices — you're predicting that their gap reverts.

Step 3: Test for mean-reversion (the unit-root test)

Is the spread actually stable, or does it drift forever? The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test answers this by regressing the spread's change on its own lagged level:

$$\Delta s_t = \gamma s_{t-1} + \sum_{j=1}^{p}\phi_j \Delta s_{t-j} + u_t$$

\(\Delta s_t = s_t - s_{t-1}\) is the one-step change, and the lag terms (\(\phi_j\)) soak up short-term autocorrelation. The null hypothesis is \(\gamma = 0\): a unit root, meaning the spread is non-stationary and the pair is not cointegrated. If \(\gamma<0\), high values get pulled back down — mean reversion.

The test statistic is \(\hat\gamma / \mathrm{SE}(\hat\gamma)\), which looks like a t-statistic but is not compared to the normal/t table. Under a unit root the statistic has its own skewed distribution, so you use special Dickey-Fuller critical values. It is a one-sided left-tail test: you reject the null (concluding stationary, cointegrated, tradeable) only when the statistic is more negative than the critical value, e.g. p-value < 0.05.

Step 4: Trade the z-score

Once cointegration passes, standardize the spread into a z-score using its mean \(\mu_s\) and standard deviation \(\sigma_s\):

$$z_t = \frac{s_t - \mu_s}{\sigma_s}$$
  • Short the spread (sell A, buy \(\beta\) of B) when \(z_t \ge +z_{\text{entry}}\) — the gap is unusually wide.
  • Long the spread (buy A, sell \(\beta\) of B) when \(z_t \le -z_{\text{entry}}\) — the gap is unusually narrow.
  • Exit when \(|z_t|\) is small — the gap has closed.
Watch out

Cointegration is a statistical relationship, not a law of nature. A merger, a regulatory shock, or a changed business model can make the relationship break down permanently — the spread walks off and never reverts. That's why a stop-loss on the spread matters: if \(z_t\) keeps growing instead of reverting, you exit and accept the loss rather than doubling down on a bet whose premise has expired.

Backtesting Honestly (and How Backtests Lie)

A backtest replays history and asks, "If I had run this strategy back then, what would have happened?" It is the single most seductive number in trading, because it is so easy to make it lie. A backtest that quietly peeks at the future, ignores costs, or only tests winners will promise riches it cannot deliver. This bot's engine is built specifically to resist those lies.

Look-ahead bias: trading on tomorrow's newspaper

Look-ahead bias is using information that was not yet available at the moment of decision. The classic mistake is deciding to buy at a bar's close and then filling the order at that same close, as if you could act on a price the instant you observed it. A subtler version is normalizing with full-sample statistics (e.g. subtracting the mean of the entire dataset, including the future) the way a z-score \( z = (x-\mu)/\sigma \) uses \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) you would not have known yet.

The fix here is mechanical: the engine decides on the close of bar \(t\) but fills the order at the open of bar \(t+1\). It can never act on a price it would not yet have seen. All indicators are causal, meaning the value at time \(t\) uses only prices \( \le t \).

Survivorship bias: only interviewing the survivors

If you test only on stocks that still exist today, you have silently dropped every company that went bankrupt or got delisted, inflating returns. Studies estimate this is worth on the order of ~1-4%/yr; Elton, Gruber, and Blake found roughly 0.9%/yr for mutual funds. The fix is to use data that includes delisted names.

Costs, slippage, and the gap between paper and reality

A system that looks profitable on paper often loses money once you pay to trade it. The engine models a commission and slippage, a small adverse price movement that pushes every fill against you, so the simulated profit is the one you could actually keep.

In-sample vs. out-of-sample

Never judge a strategy on the same data you tuned it on, just as you would never report a regression's fit on its own training points. We reserve out-of-sample data the model never saw and use walk-forward testing to grade it.

A backtest is a hypothesis, not a promise. Done honestly, it tells you a strategy might work; only out-of-sample evidence and live trading can confirm it.

Reading the Scoreboard Honestly

A strategy is only as trustworthy as the numbers you use to judge it. Each metric below answers a different question, and most can be quietly gamed if you read them carelessly. Here is how to read them like a skeptic.

How much did we make?

The simplest measure is total return: how much your account grew from start to finish, where \(V_{\text{start}}\) and \(V_{\text{end}}\) are the portfolio values at the beginning and end.

$$R = \frac{V_{\text{end}}}{V_{\text{start}}} - 1$$

But total return ignores time: 50% over one year is very different from 50% over ten. The honest per-year figure is the compound annual growth rate (CAGR), the geometric mean of yearly growth, where \(Y\) is the number of years:

$$\mathrm{CAGR} = \left(\frac{V_{\text{end}}}{V_{\text{start}}}\right)^{1/Y} - 1$$

By Jensen's inequality the geometric mean is always \(\le\) the arithmetic mean of yearly returns, so CAGR never flatters you the way "average annual return" can. It is the rate that, compounded, actually reproduces your ending balance.

How much risk did we take?

The Sharpe ratio is the classic reward-per-unit-risk measure. With \(r_t\) the return in period \(t\) and \(r_f\) the risk-free rate, it is the average excess return divided by its standard deviation (the same "spread" you met in intro stats):

$$\mathrm{SR} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[r_t - r_f]}{\mathrm{std}(r_t)}, \qquad \mathrm{SR}_{\text{annual}} = \mathrm{SR}\,\sqrt{P}$$

Here \(P\) is the number of periods per year. This \(\sqrt{P}\) scaling is exactly where people cheat themselves: \(P\) depends on your bar size. For daily bars \(P = 252\) (trading days), but for hourly bars over a 6.5-hour session, \(P \approx 252 \times 6.5 \approx 1638\). Blindly multiplying an hourly Sharpe by \(\sqrt{252}\) understates the count and mangles the number.

Watch out

The \(\sqrt{P}\) rule assumes returns are i.i.d. (independent and identically distributed) across periods. Real returns are autocorrelated and fat-tailed, so the annualized Sharpe is systematically optimistic. Treat it as a rough comparison, not a promise.

The Sortino ratio fixes one complaint: investors don't mind upside volatility, only downside. It replaces the denominator with downside deviation, where MAR is your minimum acceptable return:

$$\mathrm{DD} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{t=1}^{N}\big[\min(r_t-\mathrm{MAR},0)\big]^2}$$

Returns above the target contribute exactly 0 to the sum, but you still divide by the full count \(N\), so a strategy with many calm winning periods is rewarded.

How bad did it get?

Maximum drawdown is the largest peak-to-trough drop of the equity curve, the worst losing streak you would have had to stomach. Recovery is brutally asymmetric: a -50% drawdown requires a +100% gain just to break even, because you are now growing a much smaller base.

Reality check

Many investors abandon a sound strategy mid-drawdown. A backtest with a -50% max drawdown is psychologically unholdable for most people, even if its CAGR looks great.

The statistics of fooling yourself

Here is the most important idea on this whole page. If you try many strategies or parameter settings and keep only the best one, that best result will look impressive even if every strategy you tried is truly worthless. The number itself is biased upward by the act of searching. This is the multiple-comparisons problem, and it is the same trap intro statistics warns you about.

Recall the classic version: if you run 100 independent hypothesis tests at significance level \(\alpha = 0.05\), you expect about \(100 \times 0.05 = 5\) "significant" results by pure chance, with no real effect anywhere. A backtest search is exactly this. Each strategy you test is one more hypothesis test, and "best Sharpe out of many" is just "smallest p-value out of many" wearing a different costume.

Key idea

The maximum of many noisy numbers is not a typical number — it is systematically large. Selecting the winner from a large search guarantees an inflated winner.

How big is the illusion?

Suppose you test \(N\) strategies that are all genuinely worthless, and you measure each one's Sharpe ratio (return per unit of return volatility — here, in units of pure noise). The expected value of the best one grows like:

$$ \mathbb{E}\left[\max_{1 \le i \le N} S_i\right] \;\approx\; \sqrt{2\ln N} $$

Here \(S_i\) is strategy \(i\)'s Sharpe in noise units, \(\max\) picks the best of them, \(\ln N\) is the natural logarithm of the number of trials, and \(\mathbb{E}[\cdot]\) is the expected (average) value. Try \(N = 1000\) worthless strategies and the best one shows a Sharpe near \(\sqrt{2\ln 1000} \approx 3.7\) — a number that looks like genius, manufactured entirely by luck.

Deflating the Sharpe ratio

The Deflated Sharpe Ratio (Bailey & López de Prado, 2014) makes the correction explicit: it discounts a measured Sharpe for the number of trials attempted and for non-normal returns (fat tails and skew, which markets have). The subtlety: what matters is the number of effectively independent trials. Sweeping one knob from 9 to 11 days produces highly correlated strategies, so they count for far less than their raw number — much like correlated tests inflate your effective \(\alpha\) less than independent ones would.

Your defenses

  • Pre-register a hypothesis before you look at the data.
  • Hold out out-of-sample data the search never touched.
  • Use walk-forward analysis: fit on the past, test on the next unseen window, roll forward.
  • Correct for the number of trials you actually ran (count honestly).
Reality check

A high backtest Sharpe is not evidence of skill. It only becomes evidence once you account for how many strategies you tried, whether the data leaked the future, and whether dead and delisted stocks were quietly excluded. Until then, an impressive number is the expected reward for searching — not for being right.

Going Live: From Paper Money to Real Money

A backtest that looks great is a hypothesis. Paper trading is the experiment that tests it on data the bot has never seen, in real time, on the same code path that will one day move real dollars. Only after that should a single real cent be at risk.

Key idea

The bot uses a pluggable broker layer: swapping from paper to live is a configuration change, not a rewrite. That means the strategy code you validate is literally the code that trades. No surprises from "but the live version was different."

Why Alpaca paper first, then tiny on Schwab

Alpaca's paper trading is free, instant, and runs on real-time IEX market data with the identical code path as live. Schwab is different: the Trader API can place real equity orders, but its only "Sandbox" returns canned data and does not support real paper trading (the schwab-py library plainly states "Paper trading is not supported"). Schwab app approval also takes a few business days, and its OAuth tokens expire fast (access token ~30 minutes, refresh token ~7 days), forcing periodic re-login. So the plan is: prove it on Alpaca paper, then start with tiny size on Schwab live.

Rules that constrain a day-trading bot

The Pattern Day Trader (PDT) rule historically flagged 4+ day trades in 5 business days in a margin account, requiring \(\$25{,}000\) minimum equity. A big 2026 change: FINRA amended Rule 4210 to eliminate the PDT designation and the \(\$25k\) floor, replacing them with intraday-margin standards effective June 4, 2026, with an 18-month broker phase-in through October 20, 2027.

Watch out

During the transition, behavior is broker-dependent. Do not assume the new rule is live at your broker. A cash account sidesteps PDT entirely but is bound by settlement (T+1 since May 28, 2024): reusing unsettled funds risks a Good Faith Violation (3 in 12 months → 90-day restriction) or a Freeriding violation (just 1 → 90-day restriction).

Taxes bite too. The wash-sale rule disallows a loss if you rebuy the same or substantially identical security within 30 days before or after (a 61-day window). A high-frequency bot trips this constantly. Qualifying traders may make a Section 475(f) mark-to-market election to be exempt — consult a tax professional.

Safety guards built into this bot

  • Kill switch — halt all trading instantly.
  • Per-order notional cap — bounds the dollars any single order can risk.
  • Max open positions — limits concentration.
  • Day-trade counter — tracks trades against the rules above.
  • Explicit opt-in — required before any live trading; the default is always paper.

The go-live checklist

  1. Run on Alpaca paper until live results match backtest expectations over a meaningful stretch of unseen days.
  2. Confirm the broker layer config is the only thing changing between paper and live.
  3. Decide cash vs. margin deliberately, knowing the PDT, settlement, and violation trade-offs.
  4. Get your broker's current PDT/intraday-margin policy in writing — the rule is mid-transition.
  5. Complete Schwab app approval and verify OAuth token refresh works unattended.
  6. Set the per-order notional cap and max open positions to deliberately tiny values.
  7. Verify the kill switch works before you need it.
  8. Talk to a tax professional about wash sales and a possible 475(f) election.
  9. Flip the explicit opt-in and start with the smallest size you can.
  10. Monitor closely; scale up only after live behavior matches expectations.
Reality check

This is not financial advice, and rules vary by broker and change over time — especially during the 2026–2027 PDT transition. Verify your own broker's current policy in writing before risking real money, and consult a licensed professional for tax and investment decisions.
